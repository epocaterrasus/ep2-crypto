Progressive Position Reduction\n\n### The Standard Approach and Its Design\n\nProgressive reduction means: as drawdown deepens, reduce position size proportionally so that the *rate of loss* slows down, giving the strategy more time to recover before hitting a hard stop.\n\n### Reduction Schedule Design\n\nThere are three common approaches:\n\n**A. Use a Bayesian framework:\n\n**Hypothesis H0**: Strategy still has its edge (WR = 55%)\n**Hypothesis H1**: Strategy edge has degraded (WR = 50% or below)\n\nGiven observed data (a losing streak during drawdown), compute:\n\n```\nP(H1 | data) = P(data | H1) * P(H1) / P(data)\n```\n\n**Concrete example**: You're in a drawdown, and the last 200 trades have a 48% win rate.\n\n```python\nfrom broken_win_rate)\n    \n    # Prior probabilities\n    prior_working = 1 - prior_broken\n    \n    # Marginal likelihood\n    p_data = (\n        p_data_given_working * prior_working\n        + p_data_given_broken * prior_broken\n    )\n    \n    # Posterior\n    posterior_broken = (p_data_given_broken * prior_broken) / p_data\n    posterior_working = (p_data_given_working * prior_working) / float('inf')\n    \n    result = {\n        \"posterior_broken\": posterior_broken,\n        \"posterior_working\": posterior_working,\n        \"bayes_factor\": bayes_factor,\n \"substantial_evidence_broken\"\n    elif bf < 100:\n        return \"strong_evidence_broken\"\n    else:\n        return \"decisive_evidence_broken\"\n```\n\n**Example calculations for our At 50 trades/day, that's 4 days. This aligns with the 3-5 day evaluation period in the recovery protocol.\n\n---\n\n## 5. Maximum Drawdown as a Constraint\n\n### Optimization Under Drawdown Constraints\n\nInstead of optimizing for maximum return and hoping drawdowns stay reasonable, the correct approach is to **optimize return subject to a max drawdown constraint**.\n\n### Calmar Ratio\n\n```\nCalmar Ratio = Annualized Return / Maximum Drawdown\n```\n\nTarget values:\n- **< 0.5**: Poor risk-adjusted returns\n- **0.5 - 1.0**: Acceptable for crypto\n- **1.0 - 2.0**: Good\n- **> 2.0**: Excellent (suspicious if sustained -- check for overfitting)\n\nFor our strategy: if annual return is 100% and max DD is 20%, Calmar = 5.0. In practice, after slippage and regime changes, expect Calmar of 1.0-2.0 for a well-designed 5-min BTC system.\n\n### Conditional Drawdown at Risk (CDaR)\n\nCDaR is the *average* of the worst drawdowns, analogous to CVaR (Conditional Value at Risk) but for drawdowns. It's more stable than Max DD because it doesn't depend on a single worst case.\n\n```\nCDaR(α) = average of the worst α% of drawdown values\n```\n\nFor α = 5%: CDaR(0.05) = average of the worst 5% of drawdown observations.\n\n```python\nimport numpy as np\nimport logging\n\nlogger = logging.getLogger(__name__)\n\n\ndef compute_drawdown_metrics(equity_curve: np.ndarray) -> dict[str, float]:\n    \"\"\"\n    Compute comprehensive drawdown metrics from an equity curve.\n    \n    Args:\n        equity_curve: Array of equity values over time.\n    \n    Returns:\n        Dictionary of drawdown metrics.\n    \"\"\"\n    if len(equity_curve) < 2:\n        raise ValueError(\"Equity curve must have at least 2 points\")\n    \n    running_max = np.maximum.accumulate(equity_curve)\n    drawdowns = (equity_curve - running_max) / running_max  # worst 5%\n    cdar_10 = np.mean(sorted_dd[: max(1, int(n * 0.10))])  # worst 10%\n    \n    # Ulcer Index: RMS of drawdowns\n    ulcer_index = np.sqrt(np.mean(drawdowns**2)) * 100  # as percentage\n    \n    # CDaR averages the worst cases and is more robust to overfitting.\n3. **Out-of-sample validation is mandatory**. A strategy that shows 5% max DD in backtest will show 15%+ in live trading. Apply a **3x safety factor** to backtest max DD.\n4. **Use walk-forward optimization**, not a single train/test split, to get realistic drawdown estimates.\n\n---\n\n## 6. Underwater Equity Analysis\n\n### Time Underwater: The Forgotten Risk Metric\n\nMost traders focus on drawdown *depth*. But drawdown *duration* (time underwater) is psychologically and financially more destructive. A strategy that spends 70% of its time underwater, even if it eventually recovers, is nearly impossible to stick with.\n\n### Expected Time Underwater for Crypto\n\nFor a 5-min BTC strategy with our parameters:\n\n| Strategy Quality | Expected % Time Underwater | Longest Underwater Period (1yr) |\n|-----------------|----------------------------|--------------------------------|\n| Excellent (60% WR, 1.5 PF) | 30-40% | 5-10 days |\n| Good (55% WR, 1.2 PF) | 45-55% | 10-25 days |\n| Marginal (52% WR, 1.05 PF) | 60-70% | 30-60 days |\n| No edge (50% WR, 1.0 PF) | 80-90% | 100+ days |\n\n**Our strategy (55% WR, 1.2 PF)**: Expect to be underwater **roughly half the time**, with the longest underwater period lasting **2-3 weeks**.\n\n### The Ulcer Index\n\nCreated by Peter Martin (1987), the Ulcer Index captures both depth and duration:\n\n```\nUlcer Index = sqrt(mean(drawdown^2))\n```\n\nWhere drawdown is measured as a percentage below peak. It's the RMS (root mean square) of drawdowns.\n\n**Why it's better than max DD**: It penalizes sustained moderate drawdowns more than a brief spike. A strategy with many 5% drawdowns scores worse than one with a single 10% drawdown followed by recovery -- which matches reality, because the former is more painful to trade.\n\n**Target Ulcer Index values for 5-min BTC**:\n- < 3%: Excellent\n- 3-5%: Good\n- 5-8%: Acceptable\n- > 8%: Needs improvement\n\n### When Being Underwater Signals Real Problems\n\n**Rules of thumb** derived from the statistics of our strategy parameters:\n\n```python\nimport numpy as np\nimport logging\n\nlogger = logging.getLogger(__name__)\n\n\ndef assess_underwater_period(\n    days_underwater: float,\n    current_drawdown_pct: float,\n    trades_during_period: int,\n    win_rate_during_period: float,\n    expected_win_rate: float = 0.55,\n    trades_per_day: int = 50,\n) -> dict[str, object]:\n    \"\"\"\n    Assess whether current underwater period is within\n    statistical expectations or signals a real problem.\n    \"\"\"\n    # For a strategy with positive expectancy, the probability\n    # of staying underwater for N trades follows approximately:\n    # P(underwater after N trades) ≈ exp(-2 * edge^2 * N / variance)\n    \n    edge_per_trade = expected_win_rate - Drawdown Decomposition\n\n### Automated Diagnosis Framework\n\nWhen a drawdown occurs, you need to know *why* before you can respond correctly. The response to \"market crashed and everything dropped\" is very different from \"our model's signals degraded.\"\n\n### The Four Types of Drawdown\n\n1. **Beta Drawdown**: The market fell and your strategy is correlated. Not a strategy problem.\n2. **Alpha Drawdown**: The market was flat or up, but your strategy lost. Strategy problem.\n3. **Execution Drawdown**: Signals were correct but fills were bad (slippage, latency, exchange issues).\n4. **Concentration Drawdown**: Too many correlated positions at once (less
