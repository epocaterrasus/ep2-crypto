How Liquidation Cascades Work Mechanically\n\n### The Sequence of Events\n\nA liquidation cascade is a positive feedback loop:\n\n1. **Trigger event**: A price move (often catalyzed by a large market order, news event, or thin liquidity period) pushes price toward a cluster of liquidation levels.\n\n2. **Initial liquidations fire**: Positions whose margin ratio falls below the maintenance margin requirement are flagged by the exchange's liquidation engine.\n\n3. **Exchange takes over the position**: The exchange's liquidation engine places market orders (or limit orders at bankruptcy price) to close the position. Funding Rate Arbitrage and Its Effect on Cascades\n\n### The Mechanism\n\nFunding rate arbitrage (basis trade / cash-and-carry):\n- When funding rate is positive (longs pay shorts): Arb traders **short the perp** and **buy spot** to collect funding.\n- When funding rate is negative: Arb traders **long the perp** and **sell/short spot**.\n\n### Effect on Cascades\n\n**Dampening effect (normal conditions):**\n- Arb activity reduces funding rate extremes, which reduces one-sided positioning.\n- By adding short positions when longs are crowded (and vice versa), arb traders balance OI.\n- This theoretically reduces cascade risk.\n\n**Amplifying effect (during cascades):**\n- When a cascade begins, arb traders may unwind their positions to avoid losses on the spot leg.\n- Example: An arb trader is short perp + long spot. During a long cascade (price crashing), their perp short is profitable, but if the cascade is severe enough that they worry about exchange solvency or ADL, they close both legs — buying the perp (dampening) but selling spot (amplifying the cascade in spot, which then feeds back to perp via mark price).\n- Large arb unwinds during volatility can create \"phantom liquidity\" that disappears precisely when it's needed most.\n\n**Key insight for OI interpretation:**\n- High OI doesn't always mean high directional risk. If a significant portion of OI is arb (market-neutral), the \"real\" leverage is lower than OI suggests.\n- To estimate directional OI: `Directional_OI ≈ Total_OI - 2 * Arb_OI`, where Arb_OI can be estimated from the funding rate premium (lower premium with high OI suggests more arb activity).\n\n### Practical Signal\n\n```\nArb_OI_estimate = f(OI, funding_rate, basis_spread)\n\nIf funding_rate is near 0 AND OI is high:\n    → Large portion of OI is likely arb → lower cascade risk\nIf funding_rate is extreme AND OI is high:\n    → OI is directional → higher cascade risk\n```\n\n---\n\n## 12.
