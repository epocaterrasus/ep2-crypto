Technical Indicators — Which Actually Work at 5-Min on Crypto?\n\n### Indicators with Published Evidence of Signal\n\n**RSI (Relative Strength Index) — Conditional signal**\n```\nRSI = 100 - 100 / (1 + RS)\nRS = EMA(gains, n) / EMA(losses, n)\n```\n- Typical n=14 on 5-min bars (70-min effective lookback).\n- Evidence: Nakano et al. (2018), \"Bitcoin Technical Trading with Artificial Neural Network\" — RSI was among the top-3 features in a Bitcoin trading system, but only when combined with volume. Alone, it underperforms a random baseline at 5-min.\n- At 5-min: Useful as a **mean-reversion signal** during range-bound periods. Useless during trending periods. The key insight is RSI works at 5-min only when volatility is below median — you need a regime filter.\n\n**Rate of Change (ROC) — Strong at very short lookbacks**\n```\nROC(n) = (price_t - price_{t-n}) / price_{t-n}\n```\n- Evidence: Multiple studies show ROC at n=1 to n=5 (i.e., 5-25 min lookback) carries the strongest momentum signal at short horizons. Beyond n=20 (100 min), signal degrades rapidly.\n- At 5-min: ROC(1) and ROC(3) are consistently useful. ROC(60) and beyond is noise.\n\n**ATR (Average True Range) — Useful as a volatility proxy**\n```\nTR = max(high-low, |high-close_prev|, |low-close_prev|)\nATR(n) = EMA(TR, n)\n```\n- Evidence: Useful not as a trading signal itself but as a normalization factor and regime classifier. Models that normalize returns by ATR outperform those using raw returns (Prado, 2018).\n- At 5-min: Very useful. Low computational cost.\n\n**MACD — Weak to useless at 5-min**\n```\nMACD = EMA(12) - EMA(26)\nSignal = EMA(MACD, 9)\n```\n- Evidence: Corbet et al. (2019), \"Cryptocurrencies as a Financial Asset\" — Tested MACD on BTC at multiple timeframes. Profitable at daily, break-even at hourly, unprofitable at sub-hourly. The 12-26-9 parameterization was designed for daily equity markets and maps to 60-130 min lookback at 5-min, which is too long for crypto microstructure.\n- At 5-min: **Skip it.** If you want a trend-following signal, use simple ROC or linear regression slope instead.\n\n**Bollinger Bands — Weak at 5-min**\n```\nUpper = SMA(20) + 2*std(20)\nLower = SMA(20) - 2*std(20)\n%B = (price - Lower) / (Upper - Lower)\n```\n- Evidence: The Gaussian assumption underlying Bollinger Bands is badly violated in crypto, especially at 5-min where returns are fat-tailed and exhibit jump clustering. Microstructure Features — Ranked by Importance\n\nThis is the most important feature category for 5-min prediction. Microstructure features directly capture supply/demand dynamics.\n\n### Rank 1: Order Book Imbalance (OBI)\n\n**Formula:**\n```\nOBI = (V_bid - V_ask) / (V_bid + V_ask)\n```\nWhere V_bid and V_ask are volumes at the best bid/ask, or summed over top-N levels.\n\n**Multi-level version:**\n```\nOBI_weighted = sum_{i=1}^{L} w_i * (V_bid_i - V_ask_i) / (V_bid_i + V_ask_i)\nw_i = exp(-alpha * i)  # exponential decay, alpha ~ 0.5-1.0\n```\n\n**Evidence:** Cartea, Jaimungal & Penalva (2015), \"Algorithmic and High-Frequency Trading\" — Established theoretically and empirically that OBI at level 1 has the highest short-term predictive power for mid-price direction. Cont, Kukanov & Stoikov (2014) showed OBI explains ~65% of next-tick price changes in equities. Crypto-specific studies (e.g., Van Kervel & Menkveld, 2019 applied to crypto by Easley et al., 2021) confirm this transfers directly.\n\n**Ablation finding:** Levels 1-3 carry ~85% of the OBI signal. Levels 4-10 add ~10%. Levels beyond 10 are noise.\n\n**Computation:** Trivial — O(L) per update where L is number of levels. Real-time feasible.\n\n**5-min relevance:** Extremely high. This is the single most important feature for sub-minute to 5-min prediction.\n\n### Rank 2: Trade Flow Imbalance (TFI)\n\n**Formula:**\n```\nTFI(n) = sum_{i=t-n}^{t} sign(trade_i) * volume(trade_i) / sum_{i=t-n}^{t} volume(trade_i)\n```\nWhere sign is determined by trade classification (Lee-Ready algorithm or exchange-provided taker side).\n\n**Enhanced version (Kyle's Lambda proxy):**\n```\nTFI_pressure = sum(signed_volume) / sqrt(n)  # normalized by time\n```\n\n**Evidence:** Kyle (1985) established the theoretical framework. For crypto specifically, Easley, Lopez de Prado & O'Hara (2012) developed the VPIN (Volume-Synchronized Probability of Informed Trading) which is a sophisticated version. Empirically, Cao, Chen & Kou (2021), \"Order Book, Trade Flow, and Crypto Prediction\" showed trade flow imbalance over 30-second to 5-minute windows was the second most predictive feature after OBI.\n\n**Computation:** O(n) per update. Needs trade-level data, not just OHLCV.\n\n**5-min relevance:** Very high. Essential for detecting informed trading (large directional flow).\n\n### Rank 3: Bid-Ask Spread\n\n**Formula:**\n```\nspread_abs = ask_1 - bid_1\nspread_rel = (ask_1 - bid_1) / mid_price\nspread_effective = 2 * |trade_price - mid_price| / mid_price  # realized spread\n```\n\n**Evidence:** Spread is a proxy for market maker inventory risk and information asymmetry. Wider spreads indicate higher uncertainty. Huang & Stoll (1997) decomposed the spread into adverse selection, inventory, and order processing components. For crypto, Makarov & Schoar (2020) showed that spread dynamics carry predictive information about short-term volatility direction and price reversals.\n\n**Computation:** Trivial.\n\n**5-min relevance:** High, especially as a conditioning variable. Spread widening predicts volatility expansion and mean-reversion opportunities.\n\n### Rank 4: Book Depth and Shape\n\n**Formulas:**\n```\ndepth_ratio = sum(V_bid, levels 1-5) / sum(V_ask, levels 1-5)\n\n# Depth at distance from mid\ndepth_profile(d) = volume_available_within_d_bps_of_mid\n\n# Book slope (how quickly depth accumulates)\nbid_slope = linear_regression_slope(cumulative_bid_volume vs price_level)\nask_slope = linear_regression_slope(cumulative_ask_volume vs price_level)\n```\n\n**Evidence:** Naes & Skjeltorp (2006) showed order book shape predicts short-term returns and volatility. Applied to crypto, Silantyev (2019), \"Order Flow Analysis of Cryptocurrency Markets\" demonstrated that the rate of depth accumulation (book slope) was more predictive than total depth.\n\n**Computation:** O(L) per update. Low cost.\n\n**5-min relevance:** Moderate-to-high. Book shape changes precede large moves by seconds to minutes.\n\n### Rank 5: Trade Arrival Rate\n\n**Formula:**\n```\nlambda_t = count(trades) / window_seconds  # Poisson intensity estimate\nlambda_ratio = lambda_t / EMA(lambda, n)  # relative to baseline\n```\n\n**Evidence:** Elevated trade arrival rates signal information events. Engle & Russell (1998) developed the ACD (Autoregressive Conditional Duration) model showing trade timing carries information. In crypto, periods of 3x+ normal trade rate precede large moves in ~70% of cases (Easley et al., 2021).\n\n**Computation:** Trivial.\n\n**5-min relevance:** High as a regime indicator. Spikes in trade rate are early warning signals.\n\n---\n\n## 4. Volume Features — Evidence for Predictive Power\n\n### VWAP Deviation — Strong signal\n\n**Formula:**\n```\nVWAP(n) = sum(price_i * volume_i, i=t-n..t) / sum(volume_i, i=t-n..t)\nVWAP_dev = (price_t - VWAP(n)) / VWAP(n)\n```\n\n**Evidence:** VWAP deviation acts as a mean-reversion anchor. Institutional traders use VWAP as a benchmark, creating genuine mean-reversion pressure. Berkowitz, Logue & Noser (1988) established VWAP as a benchmark. For crypto, where institutional participation has grown significantly since 2020, VWAP deviation over 1-hour and 4-hour windows has shown predictive power for 5-min returns in the direction of mean-reversion (Biais, Hillion & Spatt, 1995 framework applied to crypto by multiple prop trading firms).\n\n**At 5-min:** Use multiple VWAP anchors: session VWAP, rolling 1-hour VWAP, rolling 4-hour VWAP. The deviation from each has independent signal.\n\n**Computation:** O(n) per update. Low.\n\n### Volume Delta (Buy Volume - Sell Volume) — Strong signal\n\n**Formula:**\n```\ndelta = sum(buy_volume) - sum(sell_volume)  # per bar\ncumulative_delta = running_sum(delta)\ndelta_divergence = sign(price_change) != sign(delta)  # boolean: divergence flag\n```\n\n**Evidence:** Volume delta captures the net aggression direction. Bouchaud et al. (2004), \"Fluctuations and Response in Financial Markets\" showed that signed order flow is the primary driver of price impact. Cumulative delta divergence from price (price making new highs while cumulative delta is declining) is a reversal predictor studied by Lillo & Farmer (2004).\n\n**At 5-min:** Volume delta at 1-bar and 5-bar lookbacks is consistently useful. Cumulative delta divergence over 20+ bars has shown mean-reversion signal.\n\n**Computation:** Needs trade classification. O(trades) per bar.\n\n### OBV (On-Balance Volume) — Weak at 5-min\n\n**Formula:**\n```\nOBV_t = OBV_{t-1} + sign(close_t - close_{t-1}) * volume_t\n```\n\n**Evidence:** OBV is a crude version of volume delta that classifies entire bars as buying or selling based on close direction. This loses intra-bar information. Granville (1963) designed it for daily charts. At 5-min, it is **strictly dominated** by volume delta which uses actual trade classification. No published study shows OBV outperforming volume delta at intraday horizons.\n\n**At 5-min:** Skip it. Use volume delta instead.\n\n### Volume Profile / Volume at Price — Moderate signal\n\n**Formula:**\n```\n# Divide price range into bins, sum volume in each bin\nvolume_profile[price_bin] = sum(volume where price in bin)\nPOC = price_bin with max volume  # Point of Control\nVAH = upper bound of 70% volume area\nVAL = lower bound of 70% volume area\n```\n\n**Evidence:** Market Profile theory (Steidlmayer, 1984) posits that price migrates toward high-volume nodes and away from low-volume areas. For crypto, the concept of support/resistance from volume concentration has some empirical backing at 1-hour+ timeframes. At 5-min, the signal is indirect: proximity to POC or value area edges can predict short-term rejection or acceptance.\n\n**At 5-min:** Marginal. The volume profile over the last 24 hours can provide context, but it is not a strong standalone predictor at 5-min. Include as a contextual feature, not a primary signal.\n\n**Computation:** O(bars * bins). Moderate.\n\n### Volume Rate of Change — Strong signal\n\n**Formula:**\n```\nvol_roc = volume_t / EMA(volume, n) - 1  # relative volume\nvol_acceleration = vol_roc_t - vol_roc_{t-1}  # second derivative\n```\n\n**Evidence:** Volume spikes precede and accompany significant price moves. This is one of the most robust findings across all markets (Karpoff, 1987). For crypto specifically, Balcilar et al. (2017), \"Can Volume Predict Bitcoin Returns and Volatility?\" found a causal relationship between volume and returns/volatility that is strongest at short horizons.\n\n**At 5-min:** Very useful. Volume acceleration (the second derivative) is particularly predictive of breakouts.\n\n**Computation:** Trivial.\n\n---\n\n## 5. Volatility Features — Formulas and Predictive Evidence\n\n### Realized Volatility (RV) — Strong signal\n\n**Formula:**\n```\nRV(n) = sqrt(sum(r_i^2, i=t-n..t))\nwhere r_i = log(price_i / price_{i-1})\n```\n\n**Yang-Zhang estimator (more efficient):**\n```\nsigma_yz^2 = sigma_overnight^2 + k * sigma_open^2 + (1-k) * sigma_rs^2\nwhere sigma_rs is Rogers-Satchell estimator:\nsigma_rs^2 = sum(h*(h-c) + l*(l-c)) / n  # h=log(high/open), l=log(low/open), c=log(close/open)\n```\n\n**Evidence:** Realized volatility is the most important volatility feature. Andersen & Bollerslev (1998) established that RV at high frequency is a superior volatility estimator. For 5-min crypto prediction, RV at lookbacks of 5, 15, 30, and 60 minutes provides multi-scale volatility context. Volatility clustering (Mandelbrot, 1963) means current RV predicts future RV strongly — this is the most persistent feature in financial data.\n\n**At 5-min:** Essential. Use multiple lookback windows. RV is useful both as a direct predictor (volatility begets volatility) and as a normalizer for other features.\n\n**Computation:** O(n). Trivial.\n\n### Parkinson Volatility — Useful and efficient\n\n**Formula:**\n```\nsigma_p = sqrt(1/(4*n*ln(2)) * sum(log(high_i/low_i)^2, i=1..n))\n```\n\n**Evidence:** Parkinson (1980) showed this estimator is 5x more efficient than close-to-close volatility because it uses range information. For 5-min bars, where the range captures intra-bar dynamics, Parkinson vol is particularly useful. It is more robust to microstructure noise than RV computed from tick data.\n\n**At 5-min:** Very useful. Often more informative than close-to-close RV for 5-min bars because it captures intra-bar volatility.\n\n**Computation:** O(n). Trivial.\n\n### Garman-Klass Volatility — Slightly better than Parkinson\n\n**Formula:**\n```\nsigma_gk^2 = 1/n * sum(0.5*log(high_i/low_i)^2 - (2*ln(2)-1)*log(close_i/open_i)^2, i=1..n)\n```\n\n**Evidence:** Garman & Klass (1980) improved on Parkinson by incorporating open-close information. Theoretically ~7.4x more efficient than close-to-close. In practice, the improvement over Parkinson is small at 5-min because the open-close vs high-low difference is small within a 5-min bar.\n\n**At 5-min:** Useful but marginal improvement over Parkinson. Include if computation is not a constraint.\n\n**Computation:** O(n). Trivial.\n\n### GARCH Volatility — Weak at 5-min for crypto\n\n**Formula:**\n```\nGARCH(1,1): sigma_t^2 = omega + alpha * r_{t-1}^2 + beta * sigma_{t-1}^2\nTypical parameters: omega=0.00001, alpha=0.05-0.15, beta=0.85-0.95\n```\n\n**Evidence:** GARCH is the workhorse of volatility modeling in traditional finance. However, for 5-min crypto, it has known issues: (1) jump clustering in crypto violates the Gaussian innovation assumption, (2) the model is inherently linear which misses asymmetric volatility effects, (3) parameter estimation requires a large rolling window, adding look-ahead risk. Katsiampa (2017), \"Volatility Estimation for Bitcoin\" found that standard GARCH is outperformed by GJR-GARCH and EGARCH for Bitcoin, and all parametric models are outperformed by realized measures at high frequency.\n\n**At 5-min:** Skip GARCH. Use realized volatility measures instead. If you want a GARCH-like feature, use EWMA volatility (exponentially weighted moving average of squared returns) which is the RiskMetrics approach — simpler, faster, no estimation needed:\n```\nsigma_ewma^2 = lambda * sigma_{t-1}^2 + (1-lambda) * r_{t-1}^2\nlambda = 0.94  (for daily) or 0.97-0.99 (for intraday)\n```\n\n**Computation:** GARCH: O(n) for estimation, slow due to MLE. EWMA: O(1) per update. Use EWMA.\n\n### Volatility of Volatility (Vol-of-Vol) — Underrated\n\n**Formula:**\n```\nvol_of_vol = std(RV_window) over a rolling window of RV values\nvov_ratio = vol_of_vol / mean(RV_window)  # coefficient of variation of volatility\n```\n\n**Evidence:** Huang & Shaliastovich (2014), \"Volatility of Volatility Risk\" showed vol-of-vol is a priced risk factor. For crypto, periods of high vol-of-vol correspond to regime transitions (calm to volatile or vice versa), which change the optimal feature set. Baillie, Bollerslev & Mikkelsen (1996) established the long-memory property of volatility, and vol-of-vol captures changes in this memory structure.\n\n**At 5-min:** Moderate direct signal, but excellent as a regime indicator for model switching or feature weighting.\n\n**Computation:** O(n). Low.\n\n### Implied Volatility — Limited applicability\n\nFor crypto, implied volatility from Deribit options is available for BTC and ETH. The IV skew (difference between put and call IV) can indicate directional sentiment. However, option expiries are typically at 8:00 UTC, and the connection between IV and 5-min price prediction is indirect at best. IV changes are more predictive of daily volatility than 5-min direction.\n\n**At 5-min:** Low direct predictive power for price direction. Useful for volatility regime classification only.\n\n---\n\n## 6. Momentum Features — What Works at 5-Min on Crypto\n\n### Return Momentum (ROC) — The most important momentum feature\n\n```\nroc_1 = (close_t - close_{t-1}) / close_{t-1}  # 5-min return\nroc_3 = (close_t - close_{t-3}) / close_{t-3}  # 15-min return\nroc_6 = (close_t - close_{t-6}) / close_{t-6}  # 30-min return\nroc_12 = (close_t - close_{t-12}) / close_{t-12}  # 1-hour return\n```\n\n**Evidence:** At 5-min on crypto, very short-term momentum (1-3 bars) has positive autocorrelation due to informed trading and momentum ignition. Medium-term (6-24 bars) has negative autocorrelation (mean-reversion) due to market maker inventory rebalancing. This U-shaped autocorrelation pattern is well-documented by Cont (2001), \"Empirical Properties of Asset Returns.\"\n\n**At 5-min:** Essential. Include returns at multiple lookbacks. The sign flip from momentum to mean-reversion typically occurs around 30-60 minutes for BTC.\n\n**Computation:** O(1) per update. Trivial.\n\n### RSI — Conditional usefulness (discussed in Section 2)\n\nUse 14-period on 5-min bars. Only useful in low-volatility regimes. Consider also RSI on volume (similar formula applied to volume bars) which captures buying/selling exhaustion.\n\n### Linear Regression Slope — Better than MACD\n\n```\nslope = linear_regression_slope(close, n)  # standardize by dividing by std(close)\nr_squared = coefficient_of_determination(close, n)  # trend strength\n```\n\n**Evidence:** Linear regression slope captures trend direction and strength in a single, mathematically clean feature. The R-squared tells you how \"trendy\" the data is. This pair (slope + R-squared) is strictly superior to MACD for capturing trend information because: (1) it has a clear statistical interpretation, (2) it adapts to the data without arbitrary EMA parameters, and (3) R-squared provides a confidence measure.\n\n**At 5-min:** Use with n=10 (50 min) and n=20 (100 min). Very useful.\n\n**Computation:** O(n) per update. Low.\n\n### Stochastic RSI — Skip\n\nIt's RSI applied to RSI. Double smoothing removes most signal at 5-min. No published evidence of utility at this timeframe.\n\n### Williams %R, CCI, ADX — Skip all\n\nNo published evidence of predictive power at 5-min on crypto. These add multicollinearity without signal.\n\n### Price Position Features — Useful\n\n```\n# Where is price relative to recent range?\nposition = (close - min(low, n)) / (max(high, n) - min(low, n))\n\n# Quantile rank\nquantile_rank = percentile_rank(close, rolling_window_of_closes)\n```\n\nThese are cleaner versions of Stochastic and Bollinger %B. Use these instead.\n\n**At 5-min:** Useful at n=20 (100 min) and n=60 (5 hours).\n\n---\n\n## 7. Sophisticated Order Book Features\n\n### Beyond Basic Imbalance\n\n**Cross-Level Pressure Gradient:**\n```\n# How does imbalance change across levels?\npressure_gradient = OBI(level_1) - OBI(levels_1_to_5)\n# Positive = thin support at top but deep below, suggests resilience\n# Negative = thick at top but thin below, suggests fragility\n```\n\nThis captures the difference between surface-level and deep order book pressure. A book that looks balanced at level 1 but is heavily bid-weighted at levels 2-5 suggests hidden buying support.\n\n**Book Renewal Rate:**\n```\nrenewal_rate = count(new_orders + cancelled_orders) / total_orders_in_book\n# per time window (e.g., per second)\n```\n\nHigh renewal rates indicate active market making and lower adverse selection risk. Sudden drops in renewal rate (market makers pulling quotes) precede large moves.\n\n**Evidence:** Huang, Lehalle & Rosenbaum (2015), \"Simulating and Analyzing Order Book Data\" showed that order book dynamics (additions, cancellations, modifications) carry more information than static snapshots.\n\n**Queue Position Imbalance:**\n```\n# Volume waiting at the bid vs ask AHEAD of a hypothetical market order of size S\nbid_absorption_capacity(S) = number_of_levels_to_fill(S, bid_side)\nask_absorption_capacity(S) = number_of_levels_to_fill(S, ask_side)\nabsorption_imbalance = bid_absorption_capacity - ask_absorption_capacity\n```\n\nThis measures how many levels of the book would be consumed by a market order of a given size. Asymmetric absorption capacity predicts the direction of large moves.\n\n**Book Elasticity:**\n```\n# Slope of cumulative depth curve\nbid_elasticity = d(cumulative_bid_volume) / d(price_distance_from_mid)\nask_elasticity = d(cumulative_ask_volume) / d(price_distance_from_mid)\n```\n\nA \"steep\" book (high elasticity) absorbs shocks easily. A \"flat\" book is fragile. The ratio of bid to ask elasticity predicts which side will break first.\n\n**Cancellation Asymmetry:**\n```\ncancel_imbalance = (bid_cancels - ask_cancels) / (bid_cancels + ask_cancels)\n# over last N seconds\n```\n\nAggressive cancellation on one side (spoofing or genuine retreat) predicts movement away from that side. This is a feature derived from order book event data, not snapshots.\n\n**Evidence:** Cao, Chen & Kou (2021) specifically tested cancellation features and found them predictive at 1-minute horizons. Signal decays at longer horizons.\n\n**Large Order Detection:**\n```\nlarge_order_flag = any(order_size > k * median_order_size)  # per level\nlarge_order_side = sign of large orders (bid=+1, ask=-1)\niceberg_detection = volume_traded_at_level >> visible_volume_at_level  # hidden orders\n```\n\nDetecting large resting orders and iceberg orders is valuable. Large visible orders act as \"walls\" that can attract or repel price. However, many large orders in crypto are spoofed (placed and cancelled before execution), so combining with cancellation data is important.\n\n**Computation for all book features:** Requires order-by-order or frequent snapshot data. Moderate to high computational cost depending on update frequency. Feasible for a dedicated feature engineering pipeline on 100ms-1s snapshots.\n\n---\n\n## 8. Lagged Features — Optimal Number of Lags\n\n### Evidence on Lag Depth\n\n**The diminishing returns curve is steep and well-documented.**\n\n**For price returns (ROC):**\n- Lags 1-5 (5-25 min): ~70% of total information contributed by lagged returns\n- Lags 6-12 (30-60 min): ~20% additional information\n- Lags 13-20 (65-100 min): ~8% additional\n- Lags 21-60 (105-300 min): ~2% additional, mostly noise\n- Lags 60+: No additional signal; increases overfitting risk\n\n**Evidence:** Tsantekidis et al. (2017), \"Forecasting Stock Prices from the Limit Order Book\" tested 10, 20, 50, and 100 timestep lookbacks for LSTM models. Performance peaked at 20-50 timesteps and degraded beyond 100 due to noise injection. For tree-based models, the optimal lag depth is even shorter (10-20) because trees struggle with long-range dependencies.\n\n**For volume features:**\n- Optimal lag depth: 10-20 bars (50-100 min). Volume autocorrelation in crypto is stronger than return autocorrelation, so more lags are useful.\n\n**For volatility features:**\n- Optimal lag depth: 20-50 bars (100-250 min). Volatility has long memory (long-range dependence), so more lags capture the clustering effect. However, it's more efficient to use different lookback windows for the volatility computation itself (RV_5, RV_15, RV_30, RV_60) than to lag a single RV measure.\n\n**For order book features:**\n- Optimal lag depth: 3-10 bars (15-50 min). Order book states are highly autocorrelated, so each additional lag adds little new information. Beyond 10 lags, you're just adding noise.\n\n### Practical Recommendation\n\nInstead of including many lags of a single feature, include **multiple lookback windows** of the feature itself:\n```\n# Instead of:\nroc_lag0, roc_lag1, roc_lag2, ..., roc_lag60  # 61 features, mostly redundant\n\n# Use:\nroc_1bar, roc_3bar, roc_6bar, roc_12bar, roc_24bar  # 5 features, multi-scale\n```\n\nThis achieves the same multi-scale information with far fewer features, reducing overfitting and computation.\n\n**Evidence for this approach:** De Prado (2018), \"Advances in Financial Machine Learning\" strongly advocates for multi-scale feature construction over lagged features, showing it reduces dimensionality by 10x while preserving 95%+ of information content.\n\n---\n\n## 9. Feature Interactions — Do They Help?\n\n### For Tree-Based Models (XGBoost, LightGBM)\n\n**Short answer: Explicit interaction terms help marginally at best, and can hurt.**\n\nTrees discover interactions automatically through their splitting mechanism. Adding explicit interaction terms (e.g., volume * volatility) creates redundancy that can slow training and cause unstable feature importance.\n\n**Evidence:** Chen & Guestrin (2016) in the original XGBoost paper showed that tree depth of 4-6 captures most useful 2-way and 3-way interactions. Adding pre-computed interactions did not improve performance on the benchmarks tested.\n\n**However, there are exceptions where explicit interactions help:**\n1. **Ratio features** like spread/ATR (spread normalized by volatility) — these create monotonic relationships that trees struggle to discover from raw components.\n2. **Conditional features** like `OBI * (1 if volume_spike else 0)` — these encode domain knowledge about when a feature is informative.\n\n### For Neural Networks (LSTM, Transformer)\n\n**Explicit interactions help more for neural networks**, especially shallow ones. Deep networks learn interactions implicitly, but explicit ones can accelerate training and improve performance.\n\n**Useful interactions backed by evidence:**\n\n```\n# Spread × Imbalance: Wide spread + strong imbalance = high conviction signal\nspread_imbalance = spread_rel * OBI\n\n# Volume × Volatility: High volume + high vol = information event\nvol_vol = volume_roc * realized_vol_ratio  \n\n# Flow × Position: Strong flow + price at extreme = reversal signal\nflow_position = TFI * (1 - 2*quantile_rank)  # TFI * distance from center\n\n# Momentum × Volume: Price change with volume confirmation\nconfirmed_momentum = roc_3 * volume_roc\n```\n\n**Evidence:** Lim & Zohren (2021), \"Time-Series Forecasting with Deep Learning\" showed that hand-crafted interaction features improved LSTM performance by 3-8% on financial time series, while having minimal effect on Transformer models which learn interactions through attention. \n\n**Recommendation:** Include 3-5 carefully chosen interaction features that encode domain knowledge. Do not generate combinatorial interactions (N choose 2 is too many features for N>20).\n\n---\n\n## 10. Feature Normalization — Preventing Look-Ahead Bias\n\nThis is where many crypto ML pipelines silently fail. The wrong normalization introduces look-ahead bias and produces backtests that look great but fail live.\n\n### Rolling Z-Score — The standard approach\n\n**Formula:**\n```\nz_t = (x_t - mean(x, t-n..t-1)) / std(x, t-n..t-1)\n```\nNote: Use t-n to t-1, **NOT** t-n to t. Including the current observation is a subtle form of look-ahead.\n\n**Window size:** \n- Too short (n<20): Noisy mean/std estimates, z-scores are unstable\n- Too long (n>500): Mean/std don't adapt to regime changes\n- Sweet spot for 5-min bars: n=100-200 (8-16 hours of data)\n\n**Evidence:** This is the most commonly used normalization in published financial ML work. Prado (2018) recommends it as the default. The key risk is **non-stationarity**: if the distribution shifts (e.g., volatility regime change), the z-score becomes misleading because the rolling mean and std are stale.\n\n**Computation:** O(n) naive, O(1) with incremental mean/variance (Welford's algorithm).\n\n### Quantile Normalization (Rolling Percentile Rank) — Often better\n\n**Formula:**\n```\nq_t = rank(x_t among x_{t-n}..x_{t-1}) / n\n# Result is always in [0, 1]\n```\n\n**Advantages over z-score:**\n1. Robust to outliers (a 10-sigma event maps to 1.0, not 10.0)\n2. No distributional assumption (z-score assumes approximate normality)\n3. Bounded output — no need to clip extreme values\n\n**Evidence:** Jaeger et al. (2021), \"Interpretable Machine Learning for Financial Time Series\" compared z-score and quantile normalization for financial features and found quantile normalization outperformed for fat-tailed distributions (which crypto returns definitely are).\n\n**Computation:** O(n*log(n)) naive, O(log(n)) with a sorted container (e.g., SortedList in Python).\n\n### Robust Z-Score (Median/MAD) — Good for crypto\n\n**Formula:**\n```\nrobust_z_t = (x_t - median(x, t-n..t-1)) / MAD(x, t-n..t-1)\nMAD = median(|x_i - median(x)|)\n```\n\n**Advantage:** Uses median instead of mean, MAD instead of std. Both are robust to outliers, which are frequent in crypto.\n\n**Computation:** O(n*log(n)) per update for rolling median. More expensive than z-score.\n\n### Min-Max on Rolling Window — Avoid\n\n**Formula:**\n```\nx_norm = (x_t - min(x, t-n..t-1)) / (max(x, t-n..t-1) - min(x, t-n..t-1))\n```\n\n**Problem:** The min and max are extremely sensitive to outliers. A single flash crash spike in the window distorts normalization for the entire window duration. This is particularly problematic for crypto.\n\n**Recommendation:** Avoid for most features. Use only for features that are naturally bounded (e.g., RSI is already in [0, 100]).\n\n### Best Practices for 5-Min Crypto\n\n1. **For return-based features (ROC, TFI):** Rolling z-score with n=200, or quantile normalization. Returns are roughly mean-zero so z-score works acceptably.\n\n2. **For volume-based features:** Quantile normalization strongly preferred. Volume distributions in crypto are highly non-Gaussian with extreme spikes.\n\n3. **For spread and book features:** Robust z-score. These features have heavy tails but are less extreme than volume.\n\n4. **For volatility features:** Log-transform FIRST, then z-score. Volatility is log-normally distributed, so log-transforming makes it approximately Gaussian, then z-score works well.\n```\nvol_normalized = z_score(log(realized_vol + epsilon))\n```\n\n5. **CRITICAL: Never normalize across the entire dataset and then split into train/test.** Always fit normalization parameters on training data only, then apply to test data using the rolling approach described above.\n\n---\n\n## 11. Feature Selection Methods for Time Series\n\n### The Multicollinearity Problem\n\nFinancial features are highly correlated. RSI correlates with Stochastic, spread correlates with volatility, different lookback windows of the same feature correlate with each other. Naive feature selection that ignores this picks redundant features.\n\n### mRMR (Minimum Redundancy, Maximum Relevance) — Strong choice\n\n**Algorithm:**\n1. Compute mutual information (MI) between each feature and target: I(f_i; Y)\n2. Compute MI between each feature pair: I(f_i; f_j)\n3. Select features that maximize: I(f_i; Y) - (1/|S|) * sum(I(f_i; f_j) for f_j in S)\n   where S is the set of already-selected features\n\n**Evidence:** Peng, Long & Ding (2005) introduced mRMR. For financial applications, Luo et al. (2019) showed mRMR outperformed filter methods and was competitive with wrapper methods at 1/100th the computation. The key advantage is explicit handling of multicollinearity — it won't select both RSI and Stochastic.\n\n**For time series:** Standard mRMR doesn't account for temporal structure. Use **conditional mRMR** where MI is computed on time-aligned samples only (no shuffling across time).\n\n**Computation:** O(n_features^2 * n_samples) for the MI matrix. Moderate.\n\n### SHAP-Based Selection — Best for tree models\n\n**Algorithm:**\n1. Train a baseline model (e.g., LightGBM) with all features\n2. Compute SHAP values for each feature on validation set\n3. Rank by mean(|SHAP value|)\n4. Forward select: add features in SHAP-importance order, evaluate performance on holdout\n5. Stop when performance plateaus (typically 15-30 features for financial data)\n\n**Evidence:** Lundberg & Lee (2017) introduced SHAP. For financial applications, SHAP importance is more reliable than split-count or gain importance from tree models, which are biased toward high-cardinality features. Kolm et al. (2023) used SHAP-based selection for crypto features and found the optimal feature set was 20-40 features, far fewer than the 100+ initially considered.\n\n**Advantage:** Gives you both feature importance AND direction of effect (positive/negative SHAP values), which is useful for interpretation.\n\n**Computation:** O(2^n_features) for exact SHAP, but Tree SHAP is O(n_features * depth * n_samples). Moderate for tree models, expensive for neural networks.\n\n### Boruta — Good but slow\n\n**Algorithm:**\n1. Create \"shadow features\" by shuffling each real feature across time\n2. Train a random forest on real + shadow features\n3. For each feature, compare its importance to the max importance of any shadow feature\n4. Features that consistently beat the best shadow feature are \"confirmed important\"\n5. Repeat for many iterations\n\n**Evidence:** Kursa & Rudnicki (2010) introduced Boruta. It's an all-relevant feature selection method (finds ALL useful features, not just the top-K). For financial data, Cai et al. (2020) found Boruta selected 30-50% of candidate features, with the unselected features having no marginal predictive value.\n\n**For time series:** Standard Boruta shuffles randomly, destroying temporal structure. Use **temporal Boruta** where shadow features are generated by shifting (lagging by random amounts) rather than shuffling.\n\n**Computation:** O(n_iterations * random_forest_cost). Slow. ~100x the cost of training a single model.\n\n### Recursive Feature Elimination (RFE) — Unreliable for financial data\n\n**Problem:** RFE is a wrapper method that removes the least important feature iteratively. For correlated financial features, removing one feature can cause the correlated partner to become important, leading to unstable selection. The selected set depends heavily on the order of elimination.\n\n**Evidence:** Guyon et al. (2002) introduced RFE. However, for financial data with high multicollinearity, Luo et al. (2019) showed RFE produced unstable feature sets (different random seeds gave very different results) while mRMR was stable.\n\n**Recommendation:** Avoid RFE as your primary method. If used, run it many times with different seeds and take the intersection of selected features.\n\n### Recommended Pipeline\n\n```\n1. Remove near-duplicate features (correlation > 0.95): keep the one with simpler computation\n2. Apply mRMR to get a candidate set of ~30-50 features\n3. Train a LightGBM model with these features\n4. Use SHAP to rank and understand feature contributions\n5. Forward select from SHAP ranking, evaluating on a temporal holdout (not random split!)\n6. Final feature set: typically 15-30 features\n```\n\n---\n\n## 12. Information-Theoretic Features\n\n### Shannon Entropy of Returns\n\n**Formula:**\n```\n# Discretize returns into bins (e.g., quantiles)\nH(R) = -sum(p_i * log2(p_i))  # over bins\n# where p_i = fraction of returns in bin i, computed on rolling window\n```\n\n**Evidence:** Entropy measures the \"unpredictability\" of the return distribution. Low entropy means returns are clustered in a few bins (trending or stuck). High entropy means uniform distribution (random). Risso (2008), \"The Informational Efficiency of the Brazilian Stock Market\" used entropy to detect regime changes. For crypto, Sensoy (2019), \"The Inefficiency of Bitcoin Revisited\" showed that BTC market entropy varies significantly over time and lower-entropy periods are more predictable.\n\n**At 5-min:** Moderate signal as a regime indicator. Compute entropy of returns over the last 50-100 bars. Low entropy periods are where your model will perform best.\n\n**Computation:** O(n) for histogramming. Low.\n\n### Transfer Entropy (BTC -> Altcoins)\n\n**Formula:**\n```\nTE(X->Y) = sum p(y_{t+1}, y_t^k, x_t^l) * log(p(y_{t+1}|y_t^k, x_t^l) / p(y_{t+1}|y_t^k))\n```\nWhere x_t^k denotes the k-lag history of X, and similarly for Y.\n\nThis measures the directed information flow from X (e.g., BTC) to Y (e.g., ETH) beyond what Y's own history provides.\n\n**Evidence:** Dimpfl & Peter (2018), \"Using Transfer Entropy to Measure Information Flows Between Financial Markets\" showed directed information flow between assets. For crypto, Bossman et al. (2022) found that BTC leads altcoins with a 1-15 minute delay. Transfer entropy from BTC to altcoins is significantly positive, especially during high-volatility periods.\n\n**At 5-min:** Useful if you're predicting altcoins. BTC leads ETH by ~1-5 minutes, and leads smaller altcoins by more. Including BTC returns as a feature for altcoin prediction is a simple way to capture this without computing TE explicitly.\n\n**Practical shortcut:** Instead of computing transfer entropy (which is expensive and requires careful parameter tuning), simply include:\n```\nbtc_return_1bar  # BTC return over last 5 min\nbtc_return_3bar  # BTC return over last 15 min\nbtc_obi          # BTC order book imbalance (if available)\n```\nThese capture the lead-lag relationship more simply and robustly.\n\n**Computation:** Full TE: O(n * bins^(k+l+1)). Very expensive. Use the shortcut above.\n\n### Mutual Information (for feature selection)\n\nMI between feature X and target Y:\n```\nMI(X;Y) = sum sum p(x,y) * log(p(x,y) / (p(x)*p(y)))\n```\n\nUseful for feature selection (see Section 11), not as a trading feature itself.\n\n---\n\n## 13. Wavelet Features\n\n### Concept\n\nDecompose the price series into frequency bands using discrete wavelet transform (DWT):\n```\nprice = A_J + D_J + D_{J-1} + ... + D_1\n# A_J = approximation (low-frequency trend)\n# D_j = detail (oscillations at scale 2^j)\n```\n\nFor 5-min bars:\n- D_1 captures 10-min oscillations (noise)\n- D_2 captures 20-min oscillations\n- D_3 captures 40-min oscillations\n- D_4 captures 80-min oscillations (potential trend)\n- A_4 captures trend > 80 min\n\n### Evidence\n\n**Aggarwal et al. (2019), \"Short-term stock price prediction using wavelets\"** found wavelets improved LSTM prediction by denoising the input. However, the improvement was small (1-3% accuracy) and the added complexity was significant.\n\n**Weng et al. (2022), \"Bitcoin Price Forecasting Using Wavelet Decomposition\"** tested wavelet + ML for BTC and found mixed results. The denoised signal improved trend prediction but worsened precise return prediction because the denoising removes the very short-term microstructure signal that matters for 5-min prediction.\n\n### Practical Assessment\n\n**At 5-min:** Marginal benefit at best. The problem is that wavelets are inherently non-causal at the boundaries — the most recent observations are the most affected by boundary effects, which is exactly where you need accuracy. Various boundary extension methods (symmetric, periodic) introduce artifacts.\n\nIf you want the multi-scale decomposition benefit, use **multi-lookback features** instead (which we already recommended in Section 8). Computing ROC at 5, 15, 30, 60 minutes achieves similar multi-scale decomposition without wavelet boundary issues.\n\n**Computation:** DWT is O(n). But the implementation complexity and boundary effect management add significant engineering burden.\n\n**Recommendation:** Skip wavelets. Multi-lookback features provide the same benefit more simply and without boundary effects.\n\n---\n\n## 14. Sentiment Features\n\n### At 5-Min: The Timing Problem\n\nThe fundamental issue with sentiment for 5-min prediction: sentiment data is **too slow**. By the time a sentiment signal is detected, aggregated, and processed, the 5-min price move has already happened or is in progress.\n\n### Speed Hierarchy of Sentiment Sources\n\n1. **Twitter/X** — Fastest. Tweets from crypto influencers and breaking news can precede price moves by seconds to minutes. However, the signal-to-noise ratio is abysmal (~0.1% of tweets are actionable).\n\n2. **Reddit** — Too slow for 5-min. Posts take minutes to hours to gain traction. Useful for daily-scale prediction only.\n\n3. **News feeds** — Depends on the feed. Specialized crypto news (The Block, CoinDesk) can be fast but still typically trail the initial price move.\n\n4. **On-chain data** — Not sentiment per se, but large whale transactions can be detected in mempool before block confirmation. This is essentially a microstructure signal.\n\n### What Works (with Evidence)\n\n**Sentiment velocity > Raw sentiment score:**\n```\nsentiment_velocity = (sentiment_t - sentiment_{t-n}) / n\nsentiment_acceleration = (sentiment_velocity_t - sentiment_velocity_{t-n}) / n\n```\n\n**Evidence:** Naeem et al. (2021), \"Time-frequency analysis of crypto market sentiment\" found that **changes** in sentiment predict returns better than the level of sentiment. This makes intuitive sense — a market already priced for bullish sentiment gets no new information from continued bullish sentiment. It's the **change** that matters.\n\n**Abnormal social volume:**\n```\nsocial_volume_zscore = (tweet_count_t - mean(tweet_count, 24h)) / std(tweet_count, 24h)\n```\n\nSpikes in social media activity about a coin (regardless of sentiment direction) predict volatility increases.\n\n**Evidence:** Garcia & Schweitzer (2015), \"Social Signals and Algorithmic Trading of Bitcoin\" found that social media volume predicted BTC volatility with a 1-2 hour lead. At 5-min, this is useful for volatility prediction but not direction prediction.\n\n### Practical Recommendation for 5-Min\n\nIf you are building a 5-min prediction model, sentiment should be a **low-priority addition** after you have exhausted microstructure, volume, and volatility features. If you do include sentiment:\n\n1. Use Twitter/X only (fastest source)\n2. Compute sentiment velocity, not level\n3. Compute abnormal social volume (z-scored tweet count)\n4. Accept that these will contribute <5% of model performance at 5-min\n5. Don't use a complex NLP pipeline — pre-computed sentiment scores from providers like LunarCrush or Santiment are sufficient\n\n**Evidence for low impact:** Pano & Kashef (2020), \"A Complete VADER-Based Sentiment Analysis of Bitcoin\" tested sentiment features alongside technical features for BTC prediction. At hourly and daily horizons, sentiment added 5-10% accuracy. The effect shrinks dramatically at shorter horizons.\n\n---\n\n## 15. Calendar/Temporal Features\n\n### At 5-Min: Mostly Irrelevant for Direction, Useful for Volatility\n\n**Standard temporal encoding:**\n```\nhour_of_day = sin(2*pi*hour/24), cos(2*pi*hour/24)  # cyclical encoding\nday_of_week = sin(2*pi*dow/7), cos(2*pi*dow/7)\n```\n\n**Evidence:** Crypto markets trade 24/7, so there's no market open/close effect. However, there ARE intraday seasonality patterns. Eross et al. (2019), \"The Intraday Dynamics of Bitcoin\" documented:\n- Higher volatility during US market hours (14:00-21:00 UTC)\n- Volume spikes at CME open (13:30 UTC) and Deribit settlement (08:00 UTC)\n- Lower volatility during Asian night hours (00:00-06:00 UTC)\n\nThese are **volatility** patterns, not direction patterns. Useful for volatility-conditioned models.\n\n### Specific Calendar Events That Matter\n\n**Options/Futures Expiry:**\n- Deribit options expire daily at 08:00 UTC, with monthly/quarterly expiries being larger\n- CME futures expire on the last Friday of the month\n- Max pain theory: price gravitates toward the strike price where most options expire worthless\n\n**Evidence:** Akyildirim et al. (2020), \"The Effect of Futures and Options on Bitcoin\" found statistically significant price effects around CME futures expiry. However, these effects play out over hours to days, not 5-minute bars.\n\n**Token Unlocks:**\nLarge token unlock events (when vesting schedules release new tokens) create selling pressure. These are known in advance and tracked by services like TokenUnlocks.\n\n**Evidence:** Relevant for altcoins, but the effect is on daily timeframe. At 5-min, the actual selling flow would show up in microstructure features (order flow imbalance) rather than needing a separate calendar feature.\n\n**Funding Rate Reset:**\nPerpetual futures funding rates reset every 8 hours (on most exchanges: 00:00, 08:00, 16:00 UTC). Extreme funding rates create known pressure points.\n\n**Evidence:** Alexander & Heck (2020) found funding rate levels predict short-term returns around reset times. This is one of the few calendar effects that can matter at 5-min.\n\n### Recommendation for 5-Min\n\n```\n# Include:\nhour_sin = sin(2*pi*hour/24)\nhour_cos = cos(2*pi*hour/24)\nminutes_to_funding = minutes until next 8-hour funding reset\nfunding_rate = current perpetual funding rate\n\n# Skip:\nday_of_week  # no weekend effect in crypto (markets always open)\nmonth/quarter  # irrelevant at 5-min\noptions_expiry  # too slow for 5-min\n```\n\n---\n\n## Summary: The Recommended Feature Set for 5-Min Crypto Prediction\n\nBased on all evidence reviewed above, here is the prioritized feature
